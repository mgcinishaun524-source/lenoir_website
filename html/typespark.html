The Problem

A Classroom Without a Computer Is a Future Without a Chance

The digital divide is not just a technology gap. It is an opportunity gap.

Right now, in classrooms across Uganda and Zimbabwe, children sit shoulder to shoulder in overcrowded rooms with no access to a single computer, no library, and no internet connection. While their peers in other parts of the world learn to code, research, and collaborate online, these students are left behind before they even begin.

Without digital literacy, young people cannot access the jobs, education, and opportunities that define modern life. The consequences ripple across generations: poverty deepens, inequality widens, and potential goes unrealised.

This is the reality for millions of children in sub-Saharan Africa. But it does not have to stay that way.

Our Promise

Every Child Deserves a Digital Future

We exist to make it simple and transparent for anyone to sponsor digital literacy, library resources, and internet access for students in Uganda and Zimbabwe, so every child can reach the opportunities they deserve.

LeNoir Foundation believes that access to technology is not a luxury; it is a right. We partner with communities to build libraries, equip schools with IT infrastructure, and deliver digital skills training that transforms classrooms into launchpads for the future.

Our promise is not just to provide equipment. It is to build ecosystems where education, community ownership, and digital innovation come together to break the cycle of inequality.

Our Story

It Started With One Computer and a Neighbourhood Full of Friends Who Had Never Seen One

When James Jay Mawaka was 10 years old, his mother bought him a desktop computer. It was a generous, life-changing gift. But it came with a weight he did not expect.

James was the only child in his neighbourhood who had ever touched a computer. While he explored games, discovered new worlds on screen, and began to understand what technology could unlock, his friends lived in a different reality entirely. Resources were scarce. A computer was not just unaffordable; it was unimaginable. They had never seen one, let alone used one.

What troubled James most was not the gap in access. It was the loneliness of it. He could not share what he was learning. He could not talk about the games he was playing or the things he was discovering. The excitement had nowhere to go, because the people he cared about most had no frame of reference for any of it. That feeling stayed with him.

Years later, after studying at London South Bank University and Social Entrepreneurship at the University of Oxford, James Jay Mawaka turned that childhood frustration into a lifelong mission. In 2016, he founded LeNoir Foundation with a simple but powerful belief: no child should have to experience opportunity alone. If one child in a community gains access to technology, every child should.

"Technology only matters when it is shared."

Our Flagship Initiative

Meet TypeSpark: Free Typing Lessons for Every Child

24 lessons. 7 African languages. Works without the internet. Built by LeNoir Foundation.

Most children in the communities we serve have never touched a keyboard. Before they can learn to code, research, or create, they need to learn the most fundamental digital skill of all: typing.

TypeSpark is our answer. A free, offline-first typing tutor built specifically for children aged 6 to 16. It uses the Byte Back methodology — the same structured approach used in schools across Africa and the UK to guide learners from their first keystrokes on the home row to full keyboard mastery across 24 lessons and 7 stages.

What makes TypeSpark different is that it was designed for the realities of African classrooms: it works completely without an internet connection, stores all data locally on the device, and is available in English, Swahili, Shona, isiZulu, Luganda, isiNdebele, and Twi. A friendly hippo mascot named Kiboko, 21 achievement badges, daily challenges, and leaderboards keep children motivated and coming back. Schools can adopt the full platform for just $30 per year, giving up to 30 students access to every feature.

How We Work

Libraries, Laptops, and Lasting Skills

We do not just deliver technology. We build the infrastructure, train the people, and stay until it works.

Library & E-Lab Builds

We construct and equip community libraries and e-learning laboratories in underserved primary schools. Each facility is co-designed with the community and handed to local operators, ensuring long-term sustainability.

Explore Our Projects →

IT Equipment Donations

We collect working laptops, desktops, and peripherals from donors across the UK and deploy them to schools in Uganda and Zimbabwe. Every device is paired with training and ongoing support, because a laptop without skills is just a paperweight.

Donate IT Equipment →

Digital Champions

Our Digital Champions programme trains local educators and community members to deliver digital literacy skills including typing, coding, and data management. This is supported by TypeSpark Africa, ensuring training continues long after we leave.

Meet the Champions →

Our Four-Step Process

01

Listen

We work alongside communities to understand their specific needs, infrastructure, and aspirations before any project begins.

02

Build

We construct or refurbish library and e-lab spaces using local labour and materials wherever possible.

03

Equip

We provide hardware, software (including TypeSpark), internet access, books, and learning materials.

04

Sustain

We train community members to manage and maintain facilities. 100% of our projects remain operational.
